**Status:** Planning

# Vision

Project Heart exists to help people create unforgettable digital experiences for the people they love.

Instead of sending ordinary wishes, greeting cards, or static websites, users can create immersive, emotional, interactive experiences that tell stories, celebrate memories, and create moments people remember forever.

Project Heart should become the world's best platform for creating personalized digital experiences for relationships.

Our goal is not to build another gift website.

Our goal is to become the platform people immediately think of whenever they want to make someone feel special.

---

# Mission

Enable anyone to create an unforgettable digital experience in less than 10 minutes without any technical knowledge.

Recipients should feel emotion, surprise, excitement, nostalgia, and joy while exploring the experience.

The creator should feel proud enough to share it publicly.

---

# Problem Statement

Current solutions mostly provide:

- Greeting cards
- Photo galleries
- Basic timelines
- Static pages
- Generic templates

These products feel transactional.

Users spend time creating something meaningful but recipients often finish the experience within a minute.

Project Heart solves this by transforming gifts into immersive interactive experiences rather than simple web pages.

---

# Product Principles

Project Heart is not a greeting card website.

It is not a photo album.

It is not a slideshow.

It is an emotional experience platform.

Every feature must support one or more of these principles.

---

## 1. Emotion First

Every screen should make the recipient feel something.

If a feature looks impressive but creates no emotional response, it should not exist.

Emotion is more important than technology.

---

## 2. Story Before Features

Users never remember features.

They remember stories.

Every experience created inside Project Heart should feel like a journey rather than a collection of widgets.

---

## 3. Beautiful By Default

Most users are not designers.

Even if someone uploads only five photos and writes three lines, the final result should look premium.

Project Heart should make ordinary content feel extraordinary.

---

## 4. Less Work

Creating an unforgettable experience should require as little effort as possible.

AI, templates, smart defaults and automation should reduce work wherever possible.

---

## 5. Mobile First

The entire product is designed primarily for phones.

Desktop support is important, but mobile experience is the priority.

---

## 6. Delight

Every few seconds the recipient should discover something unexpected.

Animations, transitions and interactions should create delight instead of distraction.

---

## 7. Performance

Beautiful does not mean slow.

Every experience should feel instant.

Animations should remain smooth even on mid-range Android devices.

---

## 8. Privacy

Users own their memories.

Project Heart should never expose private content without permission.

Privacy controls should always remain simple.

# Recipient Journey

## Overview

The recipient journey is the most important journey in all of Project Heart.

The creator builds the experience. The recipient lives it.

Everything the platform does — every animation, every transition, every interactive moment — exists to serve this journey.

A recipient who finishes a Project Heart experience should feel deeply moved. They should want to tell someone about it. They should want to explore it again. They should want to create one themselves.

The recipient journey begins the moment someone receives a link and ends when the emotional impact has settled and the recipient decides what to do next.

This document covers every stage of that journey in detail.

---

## Stage 1 — Receiving the Link

**User Goal:**

Understand what they have received and decide whether to open it.

**User Emotion:**

Curiosity mixed with mild skepticism. The recipient does not yet know what Project Heart is. They see a link from someone they care about. They may wonder if it is safe, if it is spam, or if it is just another greeting card.

**UX Objective:**

Make the link immediately feel personal, trustworthy and worth opening. The preview card that appears in WhatsApp, Instagram, iMessage, Telegram or email must do the heavy lifting before the recipient even taps.

**What the recipient sees:**

The shared link generates a rich preview card containing:

- The recipient's name (e.g., "For Priya")
- The creator's name (e.g., "From Aarav")
- The occasion (e.g., "Happy Birthday")
- A warm, branded preview image or animated thumbnail
- The Project Heart brand mark (subtle, not dominant)

The preview must never look generic. It must feel like a personal invitation.

**Success Criteria:**

- The recipient taps the link within 60 seconds of seeing it.
- The preview card communicates personal intent without revealing the full experience.
- The recipient feels anticipation rather than hesitation.

**Failure Cases:**

- The preview card looks like a generic website link with no personalization.
- The preview image fails to load, showing a broken thumbnail.
- The link text is a raw URL with no Open Graph metadata.
- The recipient mistakes the link for spam or advertising and ignores it.
- Messaging platforms strip the preview card due to incorrect meta tag configuration.

---

## Stage 2 — Loading Experience

**User Goal:**

See the experience begin loading quickly without confusion or frustration.

**User Emotion:**

Anticipation. The recipient has tapped the link and is waiting. This is a fragile moment. Any delay longer than three seconds risks losing the emotional momentum built by the preview card.

**UX Objective:**

Create a loading experience that feels intentional and beautiful rather than like a technical delay. The loading screen itself should begin the emotional journey.

**What the recipient sees:**

A branded loading screen that includes:

- A soft animated visual (e.g., a gently pulsing heart, floating particles, or a breathing gradient)
- The recipient's name displayed warmly (e.g., "Something special is loading for you, Priya...")
- A subtle progress indicator that shows real loading progress, not a fake animation
- The creator's name may appear briefly (e.g., "Made with love by Aarav")

The loading screen must feel like the opening credits of a film — not like a spinner on a broken page.

**Performance Targets:**

- First meaningful paint within 1.5 seconds on a 4G connection.
- Full experience interactive within 3 seconds on a 4G connection.
- On slow 3G connections, the loading screen should remain engaging for up to 8 seconds without causing abandonment.

**Success Criteria:**

- The recipient never sees a blank white screen.
- The loading screen itself generates a small emotional response (warmth, anticipation).
- The transition from loading screen to welcome screen is seamless with no visible flicker or layout shift.

**Failure Cases:**

- The recipient sees a blank white screen for more than 2 seconds.
- The loading spinner is a generic browser spinner or a plain circular animation.
- The loading screen displays no personalized information.
- The experience fails to load entirely and shows a browser error page.
- A large media file blocks rendering and the experience appears frozen.
- The loading screen animation is janky or drops frames on older devices.

---

## Stage 3 — Welcome Screen

**User Goal:**

Understand who created this experience, feel an initial wave of emotion, and be drawn into exploring further.

**User Emotion:**

Surprise and warmth. This is the moment the recipient realizes that someone they care about has created something specifically for them. The welcome screen must deliver an emotional punch.

**UX Objective:**

Create a cinematic opening moment that feels like unwrapping a beautifully wrapped gift. The welcome screen is the first impression of the actual experience and it must be extraordinary.

**What the recipient sees:**

The welcome screen includes:

- The recipient's name displayed prominently with elegant typography
- The occasion or a personal headline (e.g., "Happy 2nd Anniversary" or "Because You Deserve The World")
- The creator's name or a personal greeting
- A beautiful background — either a photo chosen by the creator, an animated gradient, or a themed visual
- Ambient background music begins playing softly (if the device allows autoplay and the creator selected music)
- A clear visual cue to begin exploring (e.g., "Scroll to begin", a downward-pointing chevron, or a gentle bouncing arrow)

The welcome screen should occupy the full viewport. No navigation bars. No headers. No distractions. Just emotion.

**Music Behaviour on Welcome Screen:**

On devices that block autoplay, the welcome screen should display a subtle, non-intrusive prompt: "Tap anywhere to begin with music." Tapping anywhere simultaneously starts the music and begins the scroll journey. The prompt should never feel like a mandatory step or a technical requirement.

**Success Criteria:**

- The recipient pauses for at least 2 seconds to absorb the welcome screen before scrolling.
- The recipient smiles, feels surprised, or experiences an emotional reaction.
- The welcome screen feels premium and cinematic regardless of the creator's design skill.
- Music begins without jarring the recipient.

**Failure Cases:**

- The welcome screen looks like a basic webpage header.
- The typography is generic or the layout feels templated.
- The background image is low quality, poorly cropped, or stretched.
- Music blasts at full volume without warning.
- There is no visual cue to continue, causing the recipient to stare at a static screen without knowing what to do.
- The welcome screen is skipped entirely and the recipient lands directly on content.

---

## Stage 4 — Emotional Progression

**User Goal:**

Be taken on a journey that builds emotionally, with moments of surprise, warmth, nostalgia, laughter and love.

**User Emotion:**

The emotional state should evolve as the recipient progresses through the experience. A well-designed experience should not maintain a single emotional tone throughout. Instead, it should modulate between tenderness, humor, nostalgia, surprise and deep feeling — much like a well-composed song moves between verses, choruses and bridges.

**UX Objective:**

Guide the emotional arc of the experience so that even creators who are not storytellers produce something that feels narratively satisfying.

**Emotional Arc Framework:**

The recommended emotional progression for a typical experience follows this pattern:

1. **Opening — Wonder.** The welcome screen establishes awe and personal significance.

2. **Early Sections — Nostalgia.** Timeline events, old photos, and "remember when" moments ground the experience in shared memory.

3. **Mid Sections — Joy and Humor.** Interactive games, quizzes, funny photos, inside jokes and lighthearted moments provide contrast and keep the experience from feeling heavy.

4. **Climax — Deep Emotion.** A heartfelt letter, a voice message, a video, or a powerful photo with a vulnerable message delivers the emotional peak.

5. **Resolution — Warmth and Gratitude.** A gentle closing section with a final message, a promise, or a simple "I love you" brings the experience to a satisfying emotional landing.

6. **Ending — Delight.** A final surprise — confetti, a hidden message, a song, or an unexpected interactive moment — leaves the recipient smiling.

Templates should be structured to guide creators toward this arc naturally through section ordering and prompts. Creators should remain free to reorder sections, but the default order should produce a satisfying emotional journey.

**Success Criteria:**

- The recipient does not feel emotionally flat at any point during the experience.
- There is at least one moment where the recipient laughs or smiles.
- There is at least one moment where the recipient feels deeply moved.
- The experience does not end abruptly or without emotional resolution.
- The emotional journey feels intentional even if the creator used default ordering.

**Failure Cases:**

- Every section has the same emotional tone, creating monotony.
- The experience begins with the most intense emotional content and has nowhere to escalate.
- Humorous content is immediately followed by deeply serious content without transition.
- The experience ends mid-thought without a closing moment.
- Interactive games appear at the emotional climax, breaking the mood.

---

## Stage 5 — Exploration Flow

**User Goal:**

Navigate through the experience effortlessly, always knowing where they are and feeling motivated to continue.

**User Emotion:**

Engaged curiosity. The recipient should feel like they are exploring a beautifully designed space, not scrolling through a webpage.

**UX Objective:**

Make navigation invisible. The recipient should never think about "how to use" the experience. Movement through the experience should feel as natural as turning the pages of a book or walking through rooms of an exhibition.

**Navigation Model:**

The primary navigation model is vertical scroll with full-page section snapping. Each section occupies the full viewport. Scrolling advances to the next section with a smooth, animated transition.

Secondary navigation elements include:

- A subtle scroll progress indicator (a thin line at the top or side of the screen showing how far through the experience the recipient has progressed)
- An optional section dot indicator (small dots along one edge indicating total sections and current position)
- Swipe gestures for horizontal content like photo galleries or carousels
- Tap interactions for reveal elements, games, and interactive moments

Navigation elements should be minimal and translucent. They should never compete with the content for attention.

**Pacing:**

Each section should take between 5 and 30 seconds to consume. If a section requires more than 30 seconds (e.g., a long letter or a game), it should feel like a deliberate, welcome pause — not a bottleneck.

The total experience should take between 3 and 10 minutes to complete. Experiences shorter than 3 minutes risk feeling insubstantial. Experiences longer than 10 minutes risk recipient fatigue.

**Success Criteria:**

- The recipient completes the entire experience without getting lost, stuck, or confused.
- The recipient never needs to look for a "next" button.
- Scroll progress feels satisfying and motivating.
- The recipient feels a sense of journey — beginning, middle, and end.

**Failure Cases:**

- The recipient does not realize there is more content below the fold.
- Scroll snapping is buggy and the recipient lands between two sections.
- A section is too long and the recipient loses interest midway.
- Navigation indicators are so prominent they distract from the content.
- The recipient accidentally scrolls past a section too quickly and misses its content.
- There is no indication of how much experience remains, causing anxiety or impatience.

## Stage 6 — Interactive Moments

**User Goal:**

Engage with interactive elements that feel fun, surprising, and personal rather than like required tasks.

**User Emotion:**

Playfulness and surprise. Interactive moments should break the passive scroll and make the recipient an active participant in the experience.

**UX Objective:**

Ensure every interactive element earns its place. Interactivity should never feel forced or obligatory. Each interactive moment should either reveal something personal, create a laugh, or deepen the emotional connection.

**Types of Interactive Moments:**

- **Tap to Reveal:** Hidden messages, photos, or surprises that appear when tapped. The act of revealing creates anticipation and reward.

- **Quizzes and Games:** "How well do you know me" quizzes, memory matching games, spin wheels, scratch cards. These create playful competition and humor.

- **Scratch to Reveal:** A photo or message hidden beneath a scratchable layer. The physical gesture of scratching on a phone screen creates tactile satisfaction.

- **Swipe Galleries:** Photo carousels that respond to swipe gestures, making the recipient feel like they are flipping through a physical album.

- **Shake to Reveal:** Content that appears when the recipient shakes their phone. This creates a physical surprise moment.

- **Long Press:** Content that appears only when the recipient holds their finger on an element for a deliberate duration. This creates intimacy — like holding someone's hand.

- **Pull Down:** Hidden content that appears when the recipient pulls down from the top of a section. This creates a discovery moment.

- **Countdown Timers:** Live countdowns to a future event that create shared anticipation.

**Interaction Design Principles:**

Every interactive element must include a clear visual affordance that communicates "interact with me" without using instructional text wherever possible. If instructional text is necessary, it should be brief, warm, and conversational (e.g., "Scratch to reveal your surprise" not "Please use your finger to scratch the area below").

Interactive elements should provide immediate feedback. Taps should produce visual and haptic responses instantly. Delays destroy the feeling of responsiveness.

Failed interactions should degrade gracefully. If a shake-to-reveal element does not work on a device without a gyroscope, the content should be accessible through a tap fallback.

**Success Criteria:**

- The recipient interacts with at least 80 percent of interactive elements encountered.
- Each interactive moment produces a visible emotional reaction (smile, laugh, surprise).
- Interactive elements feel natural on mobile devices.
- No interactive element requires explanation longer than five words.

**Failure Cases:**

- The recipient does not realize an element is interactive and scrolls past it.
- An interactive element does not respond to touch, creating frustration.
- A quiz or game takes too long and disrupts the emotional flow.
- Interactive elements feel gimmicky rather than personal.
- Too many interactive elements in sequence create fatigue.
- An interactive element requires a device capability (gyroscope, haptics) that the recipient's device does not have, with no fallback.

## Stage 7 — Animations

**User Goal:**

Experience smooth, purposeful motion that enhances the emotional impact of each section without causing distraction or performance issues.

**User Emotion:**

Immersion. Well-executed animation makes the experience feel alive and cinematic. The recipient should feel like they are inside a beautifully directed film, not watching a PowerPoint with transitions.

**UX Objective:**

Use animation to reinforce emotion, guide attention, and create a sense of polish. Animation should never exist for its own sake. Every animated element should answer the question: "What emotion does this motion serve?"

**Animation Categories:**

- **Section Transitions:** The movement between full-screen sections. These should feel smooth, intentional, and varied. Not every transition should be identical. A slow fade communicates tenderness. A quick slide communicates energy. A parallax shift communicates depth.

- **Content Reveals:** Elements that animate into view as the recipient arrives at a section. Photos that gently scale up. Text that fades in line by line. A letter that unfolds. These create a sense of progressive discovery.

- **Ambient Animations:** Subtle, continuous motion that makes a section feel alive. Floating particles, gently moving gradients, slowly drifting clouds, twinkling stars, softly flickering candlelight. These should be extremely subtle and never distracting.

- **Triggered Animations:** Motion that occurs in response to recipient interaction. Confetti bursts after completing a quiz. Hearts floating upward after tapping a photo. Fireworks after a surprise reveal. These celebrate moments and amplify emotions.

- **Micro-Animations:** Tiny movements that communicate responsiveness. A button that gently scales on press. A card that tilts slightly on hover. A progress indicator that fills smoothly. These create a feeling of quality and care.

**Animation Performance Requirements:**

All animations must run at 60 frames per second on mid-range devices. Animations that cannot maintain 60fps should be simplified or removed for that device class rather than allowed to run poorly.

CSS transforms and opacity changes should be preferred over layout-triggering properties. GPU-accelerated compositing should be used wherever possible.

Users who have enabled "reduce motion" in their operating system accessibility settings should see simplified or static versions of all animations. No animation should be essential for understanding content.

**Success Criteria:**

- Every animation feels smooth and intentional.
- Animations enhance the emotional impact of the content they accompany.
- No animation causes jank, stutter, or frame drops on mid-range devices.
- The experience feels polished and premium without feeling over-animated.
- Recipients with motion sensitivities can enjoy the full experience without discomfort.

**Failure Cases:**

- Animations are janky or drop below 30fps, making the experience feel broken.
- Too many simultaneous animations create visual chaos.
- Animations delay content visibility, making the recipient wait unnecessarily.
- Animations repeat identically for every section, becoming predictable and boring.
- Critical content is only accessible through animation and is hidden from users with reduced motion preferences.
- Heavy animations drain the recipient's phone battery noticeably.

## Stage 8 — Music Behaviour

**User Goal:**

Hear background music that enhances the emotional atmosphere without being intrusive, startling, or annoying.

**User Emotion:**

Music is the most powerful emotional amplifier in any experience. The right music at the right moment can transform a simple photo from "nice" to "I'm crying." The wrong music — or music handled poorly — can destroy an otherwise beautiful experience.

**UX Objective:**

Integrate music as a seamless emotional layer that the recipient controls. Music should feel like a natural part of the experience, not like a media player bolted onto a webpage.

**Music Playback Rules:**

1. **Autoplay Handling:** On devices and browsers that allow autoplay, music should begin playing softly (at approximately 30 percent volume) when the welcome screen appears. On devices that block autoplay, a single-tap prompt should appear on the welcome screen: "Tap to experience with music." This tap should simultaneously start the music and initiate the experience.

2. **Volume Curve:** Music should fade in gently over 2 seconds. It should never start at full volume. The default playback volume should be approximately 40 percent of maximum — enough to create atmosphere without overwhelming.

3. **Section-Aware Volume:** When a section contains a video or voice note, background music should automatically duck (reduce volume to approximately 15 percent) while the video or voice note plays, then smoothly return to normal volume when it ends.

4. **Crossfading:** If different sections have different music tracks assigned, tracks should crossfade over 2 to 3 seconds rather than cutting abruptly.

5. **Looping:** If the experience is longer than the selected music track, the track should loop seamlessly with a gentle crossfade at the loop point. The loop should never produce an audible gap or click.

6. **Recipient Controls:** A small, persistent, semi-transparent music control should float in one corner of the screen at all times. It should allow:
   - Play and pause
   - Mute and unmute
   - Volume adjustment (optional, can be omitted for simplicity)

   The control should never obscure content. It should be visible enough to find but subtle enough to ignore.

7. **Memory:** If the recipient mutes the music, it should remain muted for the rest of the experience. The preference should not reset between sections.

8. **No Music Fallback:** If the creator did not select any music, the experience should function perfectly without it. The music control should not appear if there is no music.

**Success Criteria:**

- Music begins without startling the recipient.
- Music enhances the emotional atmosphere of each section.
- The recipient never feels forced to listen to music.
- Music controls are easy to find but never distracting.
- Volume ducking during video and voice note playback is seamless.
- Music loops are inaudible.

**Failure Cases:**

- Music blasts at full volume when the experience loads.
- Music continues playing at full volume over a video, making dialogue inaudible.
- The recipient cannot find the mute button.
- Music stops abruptly between sections with an audible gap.
- The autoplay prompt blocks the entire experience until tapped.
- Music continues playing after the recipient has closed the browser tab or navigated away.
- The recipient's phone begins playing music through speakers in a quiet public setting, causing embarrassment.

## Stage 9 — Surprise Moments

**User Goal:**

Encounter unexpected delights that make the experience feel magical and unpredictable.

**User Emotion:**

Genuine surprise and wonder. These are the moments the recipient will remember and tell others about. A well-placed surprise transforms an experience from "that was nice" to "you won't believe what happened when I opened it."

**UX Objective:**

Embed intentional moments of surprise throughout the experience that reward attention, exploration, and curiosity. Surprises should feel personal and earned, never random or gimmicky.

**Categories of Surprise:**

- **Reveal Surprises:** Content that is hidden until the recipient performs a specific action. A scratch card that reveals a photo. A tap that triggers a confession. A pull-down that shows a hidden message. The act of revealing creates anticipation and reward simultaneously.

- **Celebration Surprises:** Automated celebrations that trigger at key moments. Confetti raining down after the recipient finishes a quiz. Hearts floating across the screen when a love letter is fully read. Fireworks appearing at the final section. These should feel generous and joyful.

- **Easter Egg Surprises:** Small hidden details that reward careful exploration. A tiny heart icon that, when tapped, reveals a secret message. A photo that, when long-pressed, shows a different photo underneath. A word in a letter that, when tapped, links to a meaningful song. These create a sense of depth and thoughtfulness.

- **Timing Surprises:** Content that appears based on timing rather than action. A message that types itself out letter by letter. A photo that slowly comes into focus. A countdown that reaches zero and reveals a surprise. These create suspense and anticipation.

- **Contextual Surprises:** Personalized moments based on context. If the experience is opened on the actual date of the occasion, a special message appears. If the recipient is viewing for the second time, a hidden "welcome back" note is shown. These make the experience feel intelligent and alive.

**Surprise Density:**

An experience should contain between 2 and 5 surprise moments depending on its length. Too few surprises make the experience feel predictable. Too many surprises create sensory overload and reduce the impact of each individual surprise.

Surprises should not appear in consecutive sections. There should be at least one regular section between surprise moments to allow emotional reset.

**Success Criteria:**

- At least one surprise moment causes the recipient to audibly react or visibly smile.
- Surprises feel personal and intentional rather than random.
- Surprises enhance the emotional arc rather than interrupting it.
- The recipient tells someone about at least one surprise moment after completing the experience.

**Failure Cases:**

- Every section contains a surprise, leading to fatigue and diminished impact.
- Surprises feel generic and unrelated to the personal content.
- A surprise element fails to trigger due to a device limitation, leaving the recipient confused.
- A surprise animation is so heavy it causes the page to freeze.
- The recipient scrolls past a surprise too quickly and misses it entirely with no way to revisit it.

## Stage 10 — Decision Points

**User Goal:**

Make simple, intuitive choices during the experience that increase personal engagement without creating confusion.

**User Emotion:**

Agency and involvement. Decision points transform the recipient from a passive viewer into an active participant. The feeling of choosing creates personal investment.

**UX Objective:**

Introduce lightweight decision points that increase engagement without creating anxiety, confusion, or fear of making the wrong choice. Every choice should feel fun, never stressful.

**Types of Decision Points:**

- **Quiz Answers:** The recipient chooses an answer from multiple options. The choice itself is fun, and the reveal of the correct answer creates a moment of humor or nostalgia.

- **This or That:** Simple binary choices (e.g., "Beach or Mountains?" "Coffee or Chai?") that feel playful and personal.

- **Content Branching:** Tapping different options reveals different content. For example, choosing between "Our Funniest Moment" and "Our Most Romantic Moment" shows different photos and messages. Both paths are positive. There is no wrong choice.

- **Reaction Selection:** Choosing an emoji or reaction to express how a section made the recipient feel. This creates self-reflection and provides the creator with emotional feedback.

- **Exploration Choices:** Deciding whether to open a hidden message, play a bonus video, or skip to the next section. These create a sense of optional depth without forcing engagement.

**Decision Point Design Rules:**

Every decision point must have a clear default action visible. The recipient should never stare at a screen without knowing what to do next.

No decision should gate progress. If the recipient ignores a decision point and scrolls past, the experience should continue without breaking.

No decision should produce a negative outcome. Every choice should lead to positive content.

Decision points should take no longer than 5 seconds to resolve. Extended deliberation breaks the flow.

**Success Criteria:**

- The recipient engages with at least 70 percent of decision points encountered.
- Decision points feel playful and personal.
- No decision point causes confusion about how to proceed.
- Decision points increase the total time spent in the experience.

**Failure Cases:**

- A decision point blocks progress and the recipient cannot figure out how to continue.
- Choices lead to visibly different quality content, making the unchosen option feel like a missed opportunity.
- Too many decision points create decision fatigue.
- A decision point feels like a test rather than a game.
- Decision points are not touch-friendly on mobile and targets are too small to tap accurately.

## Stage 11 — Ending

**User Goal:**

Experience a satisfying emotional conclusion that feels complete, memorable, and resonant.

**User Emotion:**

Fulfillment, gratitude, and warmth. The ending is the last impression the experience leaves. It must land perfectly. A strong ending can elevate an average experience. A weak ending can undermine a great one.

**UX Objective:**

Deliver a closing moment that brings the emotional arc to a natural resolution and leaves the recipient with a clear, warm feeling. The ending should feel like the final note of a beautiful song — not like a webpage that simply runs out of content.

**Ending Structure:**

The ending should consist of two distinct moments:

**Moment 1 — The Final Message:**

The last section before the closing screen should contain the creator's most heartfelt content. This might be a love letter, a voice message, a video, or a simple but deeply personal text message. This section should feel quieter and more intimate than the sections before it. Animations should be minimal. The focus should be entirely on the words.

**Moment 2 — The Closing Screen:**

After the final message, a closing screen appears. This screen should include:

- A warm sign-off (e.g., "Made with love for you" or "Forever yours, Aarav")
- A subtle celebration animation (confetti, floating hearts, gentle sparkle — appropriate to the occasion)
- The option to replay the experience
- The option to react to the experience (e.g., send a heart, a message, or an emoji back to the creator)
- The option to share the experience
- A subtle "Create your own" prompt for recipients who may want to create an experience for someone else

The closing screen should not feel like a sales page. The "Create your own" prompt should be secondary to the emotional moment. It should feel like an invitation, not an advertisement.

**Music at the Ending:**

If music has been playing throughout the experience, it should reach a natural conclusion at the closing screen. If the track allows it, the music should fade out gently over 5 to 8 seconds after the closing screen appears. The experience should end in silence — the absence of music after continuous music is itself an emotional moment.

**Success Criteria:**

- The recipient sits with the closing screen for at least 3 seconds before taking any action.
- The recipient feels a sense of completion rather than abandonment.
- The ending feels emotionally proportionate to the rest of the experience.
- At least 30 percent of recipients tap a reaction button or send a response to the creator.
- The "create your own" prompt achieves a tap-through rate of at least 5 percent without feeling aggressive.

**Failure Cases:**

- The experience simply stops scrolling with no closing screen, leaving the recipient wondering if something broke.
- The closing screen is dominated by branding, upsell prompts, or sign-up forms.
- The celebration animation is identical for every experience regardless of occasion (birthday confetti on a sympathy experience).
- There is no option to react or respond to the creator.
- The "create your own" prompt is so prominent that it undermines the personal moment.
- Music cuts off abruptly at the end instead of fading.

## Stage 12 — Sharing Behaviour

**User Goal:**

Share the experience with others or respond to the creator with minimal friction.

**User Emotion:**

Generosity and excitement. A recipient who has just had a powerful emotional experience wants to do two things: thank the creator and tell others about it.

**UX Objective:**

Make sharing effortless and emotionally motivated. The recipient should be able to share or respond with a single tap. The sharing mechanism should feel like a natural extension of the experience, not like a utilitarian toolbar.

**Sharing Options for the Recipient:**

- **React to Creator:** Send an emoji reaction, a short text message, or a voice note back to the creator. This should be the primary post-experience action. The recipient has just received something beautiful — the most natural next step is to say thank you.

- **Share the Experience:** If the experience is public, the recipient can share the link with others via WhatsApp, Instagram, Telegram, email, or by copying the link. Sharing should generate a fresh Open Graph card with a message like "Look at what [Creator] made for [Recipient] — it's incredible."

- **Screenshot and Share:** Many recipients will naturally screenshot sections of the experience to share on their Instagram stories or send to friends. The experience should be designed so that individual sections look beautiful as standalone screenshots without cropping issues or cut-off text.

- **Create Your Own:** A warm, non-aggressive prompt inviting the recipient to create their own Project Heart experience for someone else. This is the primary organic growth mechanism. The prompt should feel inspirational rather than transactional. (e.g., "Want to make someone feel this way? Create your own experience.")

**Success Criteria:**

- At least 40 percent of recipients send a reaction or response to the creator.
- At least 15 percent of recipients share the experience link with at least one other person.
- At least 5 percent of recipients tap "Create Your Own" and begin creating an experience.
- The sharing flow completes in under 3 taps.

**Failure Cases:**

- There is no easy way for the recipient to respond to the creator.
- Sharing requires multiple steps, logins, or account creation.
- The shared link preview looks different from the original preview the creator shared.
- The "Create Your Own" prompt appears before the recipient has finished the experience, creating a premature sales feeling.
- The sharing buttons are hidden or hard to find on the closing screen.

## Stage 13 — Return Visits

**User Goal:**

Revisit the experience to relive the memories, show others, or discover details missed the first time.

**User Emotion:**

Nostalgia and warmth. A return visit is a strong signal that the experience was meaningful. The product should reward this behaviour.

**UX Objective:**

Make return visits feel welcoming and subtly different from the first visit. The experience should feel familiar but still alive — not like replaying a static recording.

**Return Visit Behaviour:**

- The experience should load instantly on return visits using cached assets wherever possible.
- A subtle "Welcome back" moment may appear — a brief, warm message on the loading screen (e.g., "Welcome back, Priya 💛") before the experience begins again.
- All interactive elements should reset for a fresh experience (quiz answers cleared, scratch cards restored, reveals hidden again) unless the recipient explicitly chose to save their progress.
- The recipient should be able to jump to specific sections on return visits rather than scrolling through the entire experience again. A minimal section navigation (accessible via a small icon) should be available on return visits only — not on the first visit, where the linear journey is essential.
- Analytics should distinguish between first visits and return visits so the creator can see how many times their experience has been revisited.

**Long-Term Preservation:**

Published experiences should remain accessible for a minimum of 12 months on the free tier and indefinitely on premium tiers. Recipients should be able to revisit experiences on future birthdays, anniversaries, and nostalgic moments.

If an experience is approaching expiry, the creator should be notified in advance with the option to extend or export the experience.

**Success Criteria:**

- At least 25 percent of recipients revisit the experience at least once within 30 days.
- Return visits load within 1.5 seconds using cached assets.
- The return visit experience feels welcoming without being repetitive.
- Recipients can navigate to specific sections without replaying the entire experience.

**Failure Cases:**

- The experience has been deleted or expired without notice, and the recipient sees a dead link.
- All interactive elements remember their state from the first visit, removing the fun of rediscovery.
- The return visit experience is identical to the first visit with no acknowledgment of the recipient's return.
- Cached assets create stale content issues if the creator has edited the experience since the last visit.
- There is no way to navigate directly to a specific section, forcing the recipient to scroll through everything again.

## Stage 14 — Accessibility Considerations

**User Goal:**

Experience the full emotional impact of the experience regardless of ability, device, or assistive technology.

**User Emotion:**

Inclusion and dignity. Every recipient should feel that the experience was made for them. No one should feel excluded because of a disability, an older device, or a slower internet connection.

**UX Objective:**

Ensure that every Project Heart experience is perceivable, operable, understandable, and robust for all users, following WCAG 2.1 AA guidelines as a minimum standard.

**Visual Accessibility:**

- All text must meet minimum contrast ratios (4.5:1 for body text, 3:1 for large text) against its background.
- Text should be resizable up to 200 percent without breaking layout.
- No information should be conveyed through color alone. Patterns, labels, or icons should supplement color-coded elements.
- Images should have meaningful alt text generated from the creator's captions or descriptions. If no caption is provided, the alt text should describe the image type (e.g., "Photo shared by the creator").
- Decorative images and animations should be marked as decorative and hidden from screen readers.

**Motor Accessibility:**

- All interactive elements must be accessible via keyboard navigation (Tab, Enter, Space, Arrow keys).
- Touch targets must be at least 44 by 44 pixels.
- Swipe-based interactions must have tap-based alternatives.
- Shake-to-reveal must have a tap-based fallback.
- Drag interactions must have tap-based alternatives.
- No interaction should require simultaneous multi-touch gestures (pinch, multi-finger swipe).

**Auditory Accessibility:**

- All video content with speech should support captions.
- Voice notes should include a transcript option where possible (AI-generated transcription).
- Music is supplementary and never essential for understanding content.
- No critical information should be delivered exclusively through audio.

**Cognitive Accessibility:**

- Navigation should be consistent and predictable.
- Interactive elements should have clear affordances.
- Error states should be described in plain, non-technical language.
- The experience should never time out or auto-advance sections without user input.

**Motion Accessibility:**

- Users with "prefers-reduced-motion" enabled in their operating system should see static or simplified versions of all animations.
- Parallax effects should be disabled for users with motion sensitivity.
- No animation should flash more than three times per second.
- Ambient animations should pause when the recipient is not actively viewing the section.

**Screen Reader Support:**

- The experience should be navigable using VoiceOver (iOS), TalkBack (Android), and NVDA/JAWS (desktop).
- Sections should be announced with meaningful labels (e.g., "Section 3 of 8: Photo Gallery").
- Interactive elements should announce their purpose and state (e.g., "Scratch card: not yet revealed. Double tap to reveal.").
- Decorative animations should not generate screen reader announcements.
- The emotional content (letters, messages) should be fully accessible as text.

**Success Criteria:**

- The experience scores at least 90 on automated accessibility audits (Lighthouse, axe).
- A screen reader user can understand and navigate the entire experience.
- A keyboard-only user can complete all interactions.
- A user with motion sensitivity can enjoy the experience without discomfort.
- No accessibility accommodation reduces the emotional impact of the experience.

**Failure Cases:**

- Text is unreadable against busy background images.
- Interactive elements cannot be reached or activated via keyboard.
- Screen readers announce every decorative animation, creating a confusing audio experience.
- Videos with speech have no captions.
- The entire emotional experience is lost for users who cannot see images, because alt text is missing or generic.
- Reduced motion mode removes so much content that the experience feels empty.

## Stage 15 — Edge Cases

### 15.1 — Slow Internet Connection

**User Goal:**

Experience the content even on unreliable or slow network connections.

**User Emotion:**

Frustration if handled poorly. Patience if handled well. The recipient did not choose to have slow internet. The experience should respect their situation.

**UX Objective:**

Degrade gracefully. Deliver text and low-resolution images first. Load full-resolution media progressively. Never show broken image placeholders or empty sections.

**Expected Behaviour:**

- Text content loads immediately.
- Images load as progressive JPEGs or use blur-up placeholders that show a blurred preview before the full image loads.
- Videos display a thumbnail with a play button rather than auto-playing. The recipient chooses when to load and play video.
- Music loads in the background and begins when ready. If music is not ready by the time the recipient reaches the welcome screen, the experience should begin without music and fade it in when loaded.
- A subtle offline indicator should appear if the connection drops entirely: "You're offline. Some content may not load until you reconnect."
- No section should appear completely empty due to unloaded media. Every section should have a text fallback visible immediately.

**Success Criteria:**

- The experience is usable and emotionally impactful on a 3G connection.
- Text and layout appear within 2 seconds on any connection speed.
- The recipient never sees a broken image icon.
- Progressive loading feels intentional rather than broken.

**Failure Cases:**

- The experience shows a blank screen until all media is fully loaded.
- Broken image icons appear throughout the experience.
- Videos attempt to autoplay on slow connections, causing the page to freeze.
- The experience becomes unresponsive while waiting for large media files.

---

### 15.2 — Missing or Corrupted Media

**User Goal:**

Continue the experience without confusion even if individual media files are unavailable.

**User Emotion:**

Mild disappointment if a photo is missing, but the experience should absorb this gracefully. The recipient should never feel that the experience is broken.

**UX Objective:**

Handle missing media silently and gracefully. Replace missing content with contextually appropriate placeholders that do not break the emotional flow.

**Expected Behaviour:**

- A missing photo should be replaced with a soft, themed placeholder (a gentle gradient with a small heart icon) rather than a broken image icon or a blank space.
- A missing video should show a message: "This video is temporarily unavailable" with the section's text content still visible.
- A missing audio file should not produce any error. The experience should continue without music.
- If an entire section's content is missing, the section should be skipped in the scroll flow so the recipient does not encounter an empty page.

**Success Criteria:**

- The recipient completes the experience without noticing that media is missing (or noticing minimally).
- No error messages, console errors, or broken icons are visible.
- The emotional arc of the experience is preserved despite missing elements.

**Failure Cases:**

- Broken image icons are displayed prominently.
- A missing video causes the section to collapse or display incorrectly.
- Error messages appear in the experience (e.g., "404 Not Found", "Failed to load resource").
- Multiple missing media files cause the experience to feel empty and low quality.

---

### 15.3 — Expired Links

**User Goal:**

Understand why the experience is unavailable and take an appropriate next step.

**User Emotion:**

Disappointment. The recipient was expecting a meaningful experience and found a dead end. This is an emotionally sensitive moment. The expiry page must handle it with care.

**UX Objective:**

Show a warm, on-brand expiry page that explains the situation without technical jargon and offers a constructive next step.

**Expected Behaviour:**

The expiry page should include:

- A gentle headline: "This experience has ended"
- A brief, warm explanation: "The creator's experience has expired. Beautiful moments sometimes have a time limit."
- A suggestion to contact the creator if they want to request an extension
- A "Create Your Own Experience" prompt as an alternative action
- The Project Heart branding presented warmly, not commercially

The expiry page should never display a raw 404 error, a generic "Page Not Found" message, or a blank screen.

**Success Criteria:**

- The recipient understands why the experience is unavailable within 5 seconds.
- The expiry page feels warm and on-brand rather than clinical or technical.
- The recipient does not feel blamed or confused.
- At least 3 percent of expired link visitors tap "Create Your Own Experience."

**Failure Cases:**

- The recipient sees a generic 404 page.
- The expiry page uses technical language (e.g., "Error 410: Resource Gone").
- The expiry page feels like a dead end with no suggested next action.
- The page loads slowly, adding frustration to disappointment.

---

### 15.4 — Password-Protected Links

**User Goal:**

Enter a password and access the experience quickly without confusion or repeated failures.

**User Emotion:**

Mild excitement and anticipation. A password-protected experience signals exclusivity and privacy. The recipient should feel that they are unlocking something special — not wrestling with a security gate.

**UX Objective:**

Make the password entry feel like part of the experience rather than like a security checkpoint. The password screen should feel warm and personal.

**Expected Behaviour:**

The password screen should include:

- A personalized message: "Aarav made something special for you. Enter the password to begin."
- A single password input field with clear focus styling
- A clear "Unlock" or "Open" button
- Helpful hint text if the creator provided a password hint (e.g., "Hint: The city where we first met")
- A warm error message on incorrect password: "That's not quite right. Try again?" rather than "Invalid password"
- No CAPTCHA or additional verification (the password itself is sufficient)
- A maximum of 5 incorrect attempts before showing: "Too many attempts. Please ask the sender for the correct password."

The password screen should maintain the same visual theme and typography as the rest of Project Heart. It should not look like a login page.

**Success Criteria:**

- The recipient enters the correct password within 2 attempts on average.
- The password screen feels like an invitation, not a barrier.
- The transition from password screen to welcome screen is seamless.
- The recipient never feels locked out or frustrated.

**Failure Cases:**

- The password screen looks like a generic authentication form.
- There is no hint option, and the recipient does not know the password.
- Incorrect password attempts produce aggressive error messages.
- The password screen has no character masking toggle, and the recipient cannot verify what they typed.
- After entering the correct password, the experience loads from scratch, creating an additional wait.

---

### 15.5 — Unsupported Browsers and Devices

**User Goal:**

Access the experience on whatever device and browser they have.

**User Emotion:**

Frustration if excluded. The recipient did not choose their browser or device. They should not be punished for it.

**UX Objective:**

Support the widest possible range of devices and browsers. When full support is not possible, degrade gracefully and explain clearly.

**Expected Behaviour:**

- The experience should work on all modern mobile browsers: Safari (iOS 15+), Chrome (Android 8+), Samsung Internet, Firefox Mobile.
- The experience should work on all modern desktop browsers: Chrome, Firefox, Safari, Edge.
- For older browsers that do not support required features, the experience should display a warm fallback page: "For the best experience, please open this link in [recommended browser]. You'll love what's waiting for you."
- The fallback page should never use technical language (e.g., "Your browser does not support WebGL") or blame the recipient.
- Core content (text, images) should be accessible on virtually any browser. Advanced features (animations, games, music) may degrade gracefully on older browsers.

**Success Criteria:**

- At least 98 percent of recipients can access the full experience on their current device and browser.
- Recipients on older devices see a simplified but still emotionally impactful version.
- The fallback page is warm and helpful rather than dismissive.

**Failure Cases:**

- The experience shows a blank screen on older browsers with no explanation.
- The fallback message is technical and confusing.
- Critical content is inaccessible on any device with greater than 0.5 percent market share.
- The experience works on desktop but is broken on mobile, despite mobile being the primary platform.
